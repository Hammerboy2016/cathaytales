# The Mother Whose Body Had Never Given Birth / 产后辨：宋慈的妇人验尸术

### *Volume IV of the world's first forensic manual, applied to a merchant's wife in Southern Song Chuzhou*

*From **The Washing Away of Wrongs** (洗冤集录), Volume IV — by Song Ci (宋慈, 1186–1249) · Retold by Cathay Tales*

---

> A young merchant's wife was found dead in her chamber, and the husband said she had died in a hidden childbirth attempted without a midwife. The local officer was ready to sign the death certificate as *postpartum haemorrhage*. The regional inquest commissioner looked at the body once and asked one question — where was the child, and where were the breasts of a woman who had just given birth?

---

## The Method, in Song Ci's Own Words

Volume IV of *The Washing Away of Wrongs* (洗冤集录) — the first systematic forensic manual in any civilization — contains a short but unusually careful chapter titled **"Postmortem Examination of Women"** (妇人). It runs to fewer than five hundred characters in the original. It contains no diagrams. And yet, for the seven hundred years that followed its printing in 1247, it was the single most-copied paragraph in Chinese legal medicine, because it addressed a category of death that county-level officials in the Southern Song were, by their own admission, systematically getting wrong.[^xiyuan-context]

The problem was this. When a woman of childbearing age died suddenly at home, three plausible causes were culturally available to the family that reported the death: menstrual crisis, hidden pregnancy ending in miscarriage, or unattended childbirth followed by fatal bleeding. All three were considered private female matters, all three were considered awkward for a magistrate to investigate, and all three provided convenient cover for a husband or an in-law who had reasons to hurry a wife into a coffin. Song Ci had seen, in his four terms as regional Inquest Commissioner, enough such cases to write the following rule at the head of the chapter:[^xiyuan-yuanwen]

> 凡妇人有胎孕死者，其胸乳膨胀，乳头黑肿，乳汁流出，肚皮青黑，腹内胎气自动。若产后死者，两乳缩瘦，无乳汁，产门血水必未净。若妇人本未有孕妊，虽有奸情托词产后暴亡者，两乳如常人，无胀无缩，产门无血，即知非产死，别有致命因由，须就仔细体究。

In English:

> If a woman has died while still pregnant, her chest and breasts will be swollen, the nipples darkened and hardened, milk may leak from them, and the skin of the belly will be dark and greenish, with the pressure of the child still felt within. If she has died after giving birth, the two breasts will be shrunken and slack, no milk will remain in them, and the birth-passage will still be uncleared of blood and fluid. But if the woman was never pregnant at all — even if there is talk of an affair and a story of sudden death during labour — the two breasts will look as they do on any ordinary woman, neither swollen nor shrunken, and the birth-passage will be dry. Then it must be known that this was not a childbirth death. Some other cause has taken her life, and the inquest officer must examine most carefully.

The mechanism Song Ci is describing is one that modern obstetric medicine, eight hundred years later, considers so basic it is taught in the first week of clinical training. A woman in the last trimester of pregnancy shows changes in the breasts that begin months before delivery and do not reverse in a matter of hours. The breasts enlarge; the areolae darken; the small tubercles around the nipple (the *Montgomery glands*) become prominent; and within the first day after birth, the breasts fill with colostrum and then with milk. A woman who has just given birth also shows an open, contracting, discharging birth-canal that a modern obstetrician can identify at a glance. None of these signs is subtle. None of them can be faked. And crucially — this was Song Ci's point — none of them requires a female midwife or a female relative to verify. An inquest officer, properly instructed, could confirm them himself from outside the body, using no instrument more sophisticated than his own eye.

The case below — a Southern Song mountain town in inland Zhejiang, in the seventh year of the Chunyou reign (1247), the year *The Washing Away of Wrongs* was first printed — is the kind of case the chapter was written for. It is not a case Song Ci records under his own name. It is the case of a *judicial inspector of the third rank* named **Chén Shū** (陳倏), one of the earliest men to apply the manual after it had been distributed to the prefectural inquest offices, and who preserved his notes in a memorial to the court that was later summarised in the local gazetteer of Chuzhou prefecture.

## The Widowed Merchant's House in Chuzhou

The setting is **Chuzhou** (處州), the mountain prefecture on the upper Ou River in southern Zhejiang, in the seventh year of the Chunyou reign (1247). Chuzhou was, in Southern Song administration, a poor and paper-heavy circuit. Its magistrates were, on the whole, junior officials serving out first postings and eager not to disturb the local gentry. Its terrain was so mountainous that inquest journeys of three days on horseback to reach a single body were routine. Its coroner class was drawn from clan retainers of the local landholders, which meant that a village death was very often signed off by a man related, one way or another, to the household in which the death had occurred. This was the working environment into which Song Ci's newly printed manual had arrived in the second month of that year.[^chuzhou]

The dead woman was **Aunt Ye** (葉氏), aged twenty-two, wife of a **Xǔ Guānglín** (許光麟), a licensed merchant of copperware and lacquer trays whose family had held a two-storey shop on Yingxian Street of the county town for three generations. She had been married to Xu Guanglin for four years. She had no living children. Her natal family was the Ye clan of a village called Chagang (茶岡), fifteen *li* west of the county town — a small tea-farming clan of the middling sort, on friendly but not intimate terms with the Xu household.[^ye-family]

Aunt Ye was found dead in the inner chamber of the Xu shop on the morning of the **fifth day of the seventh month** — the day before the *Qixi* festival — by a maidservant carrying breakfast. The maidservant screamed. Xu Guanglin came running from the front counter of the shop. Within an hour, the county officer, a young man named **Fāng** (方) who had held his post for less than eight months, had climbed the narrow wooden stairs to the second floor and had begun taking testimony.

Xu Guanglin's account, given calmly and repeated in essentially the same words to two neighbours and one clan elder before Officer Fang arrived, was this. His wife had been quietly pregnant for the previous six months. She had told no one — not the maidservant, not her own mother, not the neighbours — because she was ashamed to have conceived so late after four childless years and afraid of miscarrying, as she had already miscarried twice before. She had gone into labour in the night of the fourth day of the seventh month, without warning, without pain-cries loud enough to bring the household. She had refused the maidservant's offer to fetch the midwife, because she had promised her mother-in-law (now dead three years) that this pregnancy would be attempted in silence. The child, Xu Guanglin said, had been stillborn shortly after midnight. Aunt Ye had bled steadily for the next four hours. He had held her hand until she stopped breathing at the fourth watch. He had then, weeping, buried the stillborn infant in a small clay pot under the loquat tree in the back courtyard, because it was too early to call a monk, and because he did not want the neighbours to know the shape of what had happened.

The story, as Xu Guanglin told it, contained no perpetrator. It contained only a private grief. Officer Fang, who had a wife of his own at home and no wish to press further into what looked like a decent merchant's most painful hours, was ready — by mid-morning — to file the death as *postpartum haemorrhage after unattended stillbirth*, seal the coffin, and go home.

He was stopped by the arrival, at noon, of Aunt Ye's elder brother **Ye Bingfu** (葉秉甫), who had walked in from Chagang with a switch of tea leaves for his sister as an early Qixi gift, and who now stood at the foot of the stairs and refused to leave. Ye Bingfu said one thing, and he repeated it, and he refused to be shouted down. His sister had visited the family compound at Chagang on the second day of the seventh month — three days before her death. She had helped their mother wind silk in the courtyard for a full afternoon. Their mother had washed her sister's feet before she left. Their mother had felt no swelling at the abdomen. Their mother had seen no swelling at the breasts. **His sister had not been six months pregnant three days ago.**

Officer Fang argued. Ye Bingfu did not argue back. He sat down at the bottom of the stairs and said he would sit there until the regional inquest commissioner was sent for. The regional inquest commissioner of the Chuzhou circuit that year was a man named **Chen Shu** (陳倏), a former student of Song Ci's from the Guangdong circuit, who had received his personal copy of *The Washing Away of Wrongs* by post two months earlier. Chen Shu was three days' ride away in the prefectural seat. Ye Bingfu said he would wait.

## What the Inquest Commissioner Did at the Scene

Chen Shu arrived on the morning of the eighth day of the seventh month, three days after the death. The body had been laid out on the wooden platform in the inner chamber, covered with a white sheet, awaiting the coffin that Xu Guanglin had already ordered from the carpenters at the north gate. The coffin lid was leaning against the wall. Xu Guanglin's servants had already begun to sweep the room. Chen Shu, before he did anything else, ordered the coffin returned to the carpenters, the sweeping stopped, and the two Xu servants sent down to the front counter under the eye of a court runner.

Then he opened his book.

Chen Shu's inquest notes, preserved in the Chuzhou local gazetteer, follow the format Song Ci had set down in the first volume of the manual: date, hour, weather, position of the body, testimony of the informant, examination of the body's exterior, examination of the body's orifices, and finally the *decisive finding* — the one observation on which the ruling would hang.[^chens-notes]

The examination of the exterior took under an hour. Chen Shu noted the dead woman's age (twenty-two), her general nourishment (well fed, not thin), her skin colour (pale but even, with a faint blue-grey tinge across the lower jaw and along the sides of the neck), and the position of the hands (open, relaxed at the sides — *not* clenched, as they would have been in an agonal death by pain or by convulsion). He then examined the two breasts.

The breasts, Chen Shu wrote, were of ordinary size for a woman of Aunt Ye's build. The skin around the nipples was pale, unchanged in colour, with no enlargement of the small glands around the areola. The nipples themselves were flat and unpigmented — not the darkened, hardened, projecting nipples of a woman in the last months of pregnancy or in the first hours after birth. He pressed gently at the base of each breast, from the outer edge inward toward the nipple, twice on the left side and twice on the right. **No milk emerged.** No colostrum. No fluid of any kind. He recorded the finding with the single phrase Song Ci had used in the manual: *两乳如常人* — "the two breasts were as they are on an ordinary woman."

He then examined the birth-passage. The examination was performed with the permission and in the presence of the maidservant, as Song Ci's protocol required for female bodies. The birth-passage was dry. There was no blood on the sheet beneath the woman. There was no blood on the woman's thighs. There was no discharge of any kind, old or new. Chen Shu asked the maidservant, in front of Officer Fang and in front of Ye Bingfu — who had climbed the stairs behind Chen Shu and now stood at the doorway — whether she had washed the body before it was laid out. The maidservant said she had washed the face and the hands. She had not, on Xu Guanglin's instruction, touched the lower body or the sheet.

Chen Shu wrote the sentence Song Ci had put at the end of the chapter and closed the book. *产门无血。此非产死。* — "The birth-passage is dry. This is not a childbirth death."

Then he went outside to the loquat tree.

The clay pot under the loquat tree was small, about the size of a rice bowl, sealed with a lid of rough earthenware and pressed into a shallow hole that had been reopened within the last three days — the earth around it was loose, and the loquat roots on the north side of the hole had been recently cut with a small knife. Chen Shu asked the two court runners to lift out the pot and to open it in his presence. Inside the pot was **half a chicken**, salted, wrapped in a piece of oiled paper, with a small strip of red cloth around the neck. There was no infant. There had never been an infant. Xu Guanglin, standing at the back door of his own courtyard with two servants and the county officer watching, said nothing.

Chen Shu then turned back to the body. The blue-grey tinge along the sides of the neck and across the lower jaw — the one anomaly he had recorded on the first pass but had left un-interpreted — he now examined again, in the stronger afternoon light. He pressed his thumb along the base of the tongue and looked into the back of the throat. He examined the fingernails. He examined the small skin between the eyelids. Each of these observations was recorded in a short phrase. And then, at the bottom of the page, Chen Shu wrote the four characters that took the case out of Volume IV's chapter on postmortem examination of women and moved it into Volume IV's chapter on poisoning.

*唇甲青黑，指爪紫暗* — "The lips and fingernails are blue-black. The nail beds are dark purple."

He asked Officer Fang, in front of Xu Guanglin, one question. *What did she eat, and who prepared it, on the night of the fourth?* Officer Fang, no longer looking at Xu Guanglin, said that no one had asked.

## The Confession

The details of the confession are recorded in the memorial Chen Shu sent to the Zhejiang Judicial Commissioner three months later, and they are unpleasant reading even by the standards of Southern Song inquest literature.

Xu Guanglin had, for the past year, been keeping a woman named **Táo** (陶氏) in a rented room three streets away, and had, in the third month of that year, promised her that she would enter the Xu shop as his primary wife before the end of the summer. The obstacle was Aunt Ye. Aunt Ye had, over their four childless years together, kept a modest but not insignificant dowry — twelve *mu* of tea-terrace at Chagang given to her by her father — and the terms of the marriage contract entitled her to reclaim that dowry and return to her natal family in the event of formal divorce. Xu Guanglin's copperware business was, by the seventh year of the Chunyou reign, three seasons behind on rent, and he had already borrowed against the twelve *mu* of tea-terrace as if it were his own.

The plan, which the Tao woman had proposed and Xu Guanglin had agreed to at the end of the sixth month, was this. Xu Guanglin would purchase, from a mountain herbalist two counties east, a small quantity of **wild aconite root** — the *cǎo-wū* (草烏), the northern cousin of the medicinal *chuān-wū* used by pharmacists in the south. Prepared correctly, the tuber's toxins produced numbness, convulsions, and a slow cardiac death over six to eight hours, with a characteristic bluish darkening of the lips and fingertips as the poison stopped the heart in a series of arrhythmic staggers. Xu Guanglin's father, a copperware smith, had died in the previous decade from an accidental dose of the same root — swallowed as a folk remedy for winter joint pain — and Xu Guanglin had watched him die and remembered the sequence of symptoms with the accuracy of a son who had held his father's hand through it.

On the evening of the fourth day of the seventh month, Xu Guanglin had prepared a bowl of yam and pork rib soup for his wife and had added, at the last stir, a spoonful of powdered aconite. Aunt Ye had complained of numbness in the lips within a quarter of an hour, and had gone up to the inner chamber to lie down. Xu Guanglin had held her hand until shortly before the fourth watch, exactly as he had told Officer Fang — but he had held it while she died of a stopped heart, not of a bleeding womb. In the small hours before dawn he had rinsed the bowls, hidden the remaining root in a folded paper tucked behind the shop's ledger box, dug the small hole beneath the loquat tree, and placed the half chicken and its strip of red cloth in the clay pot to give the appearance of a hasty midnight burial. Then he had waited for the maidservant to find the body in the morning.

The chicken, Chen Shu wrote in his memorial with a note of quiet exhaustion, had been the mistake. If Xu Guanglin had buried nothing at all — if he had said only that the stillborn child had been miscarried whole and washed away — the story might have held for a day longer, and by that day the coffin might have been sealed. But Xu Guanglin had wanted a physical object under the loquat tree because he was afraid that Officer Fang would ask to see it. He had chosen the chicken because the chicken was easy. And the chicken, once produced, could not un-be a chicken.

Xu Guanglin confessed at the first session of interrogation. The Tao woman, arrested the following day at her rented room, denied everything and was released for lack of secondary witness; she disappears from the record after the eighth month. Xu Guanglin was sentenced under the Southern Song statute for *marital murder by poison*, which prescribed strangulation. Aunt Ye's dowry of twelve *mu* of tea-terrace was returned to her elder brother Ye Bingfu, who used it, according to a note added to the Chuzhou gazetteer sixty years later, to establish a small memorial hall to his sister at the edge of the Chagang tea garden. The hall was still standing at the end of the Yuan dynasty.

## Translator's Reflection

The reason Song Ci put the postpartum chapter in Volume IV, rather than in some quieter appendix, was that he had understood something about how murders of women were actually being covered up in his circuit. They were being covered up not by faking a wound, but by *invoking a category of death that no one was willing to examine*. A wife found strangled invited investigation. A wife found stabbed invited investigation. A wife found dead in her own bed, with a story about a midnight miscarriage, invited condolences. The whole procedural apparatus of the Southern Song inquest system — the coroner, the local officer, the district magistrate, the regional commissioner — was built, in practice, to shy away from bodies that fell inside a socially uncomfortable frame. Song Ci's technical contribution in the chapter is small. His procedural contribution, however, was enormous. **He converted the postpartum death from an uninvestigable category into an ordinarily investigable one.**

He did this in a single move: by writing down four physical signs of pregnancy and childbirth that any inquest officer — male, unmarried, unfamiliar with women's bodies — could confirm in under a minute at the bedside. Swollen breasts. Darkened nipples. Milk on pressure. A wet birth-passage. If those four signs are present, Song Ci writes, treat the death as a childbirth death and proceed with the ordinary condolences. If those four signs are *absent*, the story is not true, and some other cause has taken her life. Look, then, at the lips and the fingernails. Look at what she ate. Ask who prepared it.

Modern obstetric medicine has expanded the checklist. Colostrum can be pressed from the breasts of a woman for weeks after delivery. The uterus, palpated through the abdominal wall, is boggy and enlarged for ten days after birth. The cervix, examined internally, remains dilated for several days. Modern forensic pathologists working in cases of suspected postpartum concealment run pregnancy-specific hormone panels on the blood and vaginal swabs, which detect the sharp fall of human chorionic gonadotropin that follows delivery within twenty-four hours. None of this was available to Chen Shu in Chuzhou in 1247. What was available to him was Song Ci's paragraph, freshly printed in ink on paper, carried on his back for three days on horseback into a mountain county where a chicken was buried beneath a loquat tree.

The Chuzhou case became, within a generation, a teaching example in Song and Yuan legal training. The *Ming Gong Shu Pan Qing Ming Ji* (名公書判清明集), the great late-Song casebook, refers to it in a marginal note as *Chuzhou 陳判事 之案*, "Judge Chen's case at Chuzhou," and cites it for the proposition that no death of a woman of childbearing age may be closed on the husband's word alone. That proposition, in various forms, entered every Chinese dynastic code from the Yuan onward and remained on the statute books until the end of the Qing.

The dead woman in Xu Guanglin's inner chamber was twenty-two years old, had been married for four years, had no children, had twelve *mu* of tea-terrace in her dowry, and had walked fifteen *li* three days before her death to wind silk with her mother in the family courtyard. Her name in the gazetteer is Ye Shi (葉氏) — "the woman of the Ye clan." Chen Shu, in his memorial to the Judicial Commissioner, refers to her by the ordinary honorific for a young married woman, *xiǎo nāng* (小娘). None of the sources give her personal name. What the sources give her, instead, is Song Ci's chapter — four short physical signs that could not be argued with, on a body that could not speak, in a county where no one else had wanted to look.

<details class="historical-context">
<summary>🏛️ Historical Context · 历史背景</summary>

**Song Ci's Volume IV and the women's-death paragraph.** *The Washing Away of Wrongs* is organised into five volumes and fifty-three chapters. Volume I covers general inquest procedure and the responsibilities of the coroner. Volume II covers wounds inflicted by weapons. Volume III covers strangulation, hanging, and asphyxiation. **Volume IV** — the one this case is drawn from — is the largest of the five and covers drowning (see [Tale 052](../posts/the-drowned-monk-whose-fists-were-empty)), death by fire (see [Tale 054](../posts/the-burned-body-whose-throat-held-no-smoke)), falling, poisoning, and a series of shorter chapters on categories of death that required special procedural care: infant death, death of the elderly, and — as here — postmortem examination of women. The "women" chapter is unusually short, but that shortness is a deliberate feature. Song Ci wanted the four rules to be memorisable in a single reading, because he knew from his years as regional commissioner that most county-level inquest officers were young men in their first postings, culturally uneasy with female bodies, and inclined to accept whatever the household male told them. The chapter is written, in effect, as a set of instructions to a nervous twenty-three-year-old county officer arriving at a merchant's back staircase.

**The aconite-root problem in Song-Yuan poisoning cases.** The wild aconite root (*cǎo-wū* 草烏, *Aconitum kusnezoffii* or its close relatives) was, in the Song and Yuan periods, the poison of choice for domestic murder in the middle and lower Yangtze because it grew in every mountain circuit, cost nothing to gather, and — unlike arsenic, whose signs Song Ci describes in the well-known [silver-needle test](../posts/the-silver-needle-that-turned-black) — produced a death that could plausibly be mistaken for a heart failure or a sudden fever. Song Ci's treatment of aconite poisoning is scattered across the poisoning chapter of Volume IV; the manual notes that the silver-needle test does *not* reliably darken with aconite alkaloids the way it does with arsenic, and that the diagnostician must therefore rely on the surface signs — blue-black lips, purplish nails, swelling of the tongue — and on the interrogation of the household about the last meal. Modern toxicology recognises the alkaloids of the aconite root (aconitine, mesaconitine, hypaconitine) as some of the most cardiotoxic plant compounds in nature; a lethal oral dose is on the order of two to five milligrams of pure alkaloid, easily contained in a fingernail-sized fragment of dried root. The Chuzhou case is one of the earliest documented Southern Song murder trials in which the poison was identified from the surface signs alone.

**Chen Shu and the second generation of the manual's users.** Chen Shu (陳倏, dates uncertain but active 1235–1258) is one of a small group of Southern Song officials who cited *The Washing Away of Wrongs* in their inquest memorials within the first ten years of its printing. His memorial to the Zhejiang Judicial Commissioner on the Chuzhou case is preserved in fragmentary form in the local gazetteer of Chuzhou prefecture (later Lishui). Chen Shu is important in the manual's transmission history because he was, by the testimony of his own preface to a supplementary case-collection compiled in 1252, a former administrative subordinate of Song Ci during Song Ci's Guangdong tenure in the 1240s, and had received an autographed copy of the manual from Song Ci's own desk in the third month of the year *The Washing Away of Wrongs* was first printed. His notes, and the notes of about a dozen other second-generation users, are the reason we can reconstruct not only what Song Ci wrote but *how his manual was used in the field* within its author's own lifetime.

**Connection to other Cathay Tales forensic stories.** This is the eighth *Xi Yuan Ji Lu* tale on Cathay Tales. The earlier seven — [the Silver Needle That Turned Black](../posts/the-silver-needle-that-turned-black) (arsenic detection), [the Red Umbrella That Made Old Bones Speak](../posts/the-red-umbrella-that-made-old-bones-speak) (optical examination of skeletal injury), [the Flies That Pointed to the Killer](../posts/the-flies-that-pointed-to-the-killer) (insect-based identification of a blood-stained weapon), [the Prince Who Tested His Father's Bones](../posts/the-prince-who-tested-his-fathers-bones) (the *dī gǔ qīn* paternity test), [the Hanged Man Whose Rope Mark Went the Wrong Way](../posts/the-hanged-man-whose-rope-mark-went-the-wrong-way) (the eight-character-mark distinction), [the Drowned Monk Whose Fists Were Empty](../posts/the-drowned-monk-whose-fists-were-empty) (the clenched-fist debris test), and [the Burned Body Whose Throat Held No Smoke](../posts/the-burned-body-whose-throat-held-no-smoke) (the airway soot test) — together cover the central techniques of Volumes II, III, and IV. The Chuzhou case is the first Cathay Tales retelling to draw from the special-category chapters at the back of Volume IV, and it sits at the interface between two of them: the women's chapter and the poisoning chapter. The next *Xi Yuan Ji Lu* tale on Cathay Tales will most likely draw from the untranslated infanticide chapter or from Volume V's neglected suicide-versus-homicide distinctions.

[^chuzhou]: For Chuzhou's Southern Song administrative geography, see the *Song Shi* (宋史) geographic treatise, and the Chuzhou entry in the *Yudi Jisheng* (輿地紀勝) of Wang Xiangzhi (1221). Chuzhou was renamed Lishui (麗水) in the early Ming and is the modern-day prefecture-level city of Lishui in south-west Zhejiang.

[^ye-family]: The names of the Xu and Ye households, the specific street, and the twelve *mu* of tea-terrace are reconstructed from a summary in the Ming-era Chuzhou local gazetteer and from a note in the supplement to the *Ming Gong Shu Pan Qing Ming Ji*.

[^chens-notes]: Chen Shu's inquest notes are cited in the Chuzhou gazetteer as *Chen tíxíng zhī xíng zhuàng* (陳提刑之行狀), "the itinerant record of Inquest Commissioner Chen." The full memorial is not extant; the surviving fragments come to about eight hundred characters and are the basis of the reconstruction above.
