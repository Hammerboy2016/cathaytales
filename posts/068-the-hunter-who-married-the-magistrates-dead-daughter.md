# The Hunter Who Married the Magistrate's Dead Daughter / 卢充幽婚

### *A Han-dynasty deer hunt, a manor that wasn't there, a letter in a dead father's hand, and a son delivered from the far side of the grave*

*From **In Search of the Supernatural** (搜神记), Book XVI — by Gan Bao (干宝, c. 286–336) · Retold by Cathay Tales*

---

> Lu Chong of Fanyang was twenty years old when he chased a wounded deer into a manor that should not have existed. Inside, a court official named Cui Shaofu produced a letter — written, unmistakably, in the hand of Lu Chong's own long-dead father — arranging a marriage between Lu Chong and Cui's daughter. Lu Chong stayed three nights. He was told that if a son was born, the son would be returned. Then he was sent home. On the road he realized that the manor had been a tomb. Four years later, on the third day of the third month, the daughter came back to him at the waterside — with a three-year-old child, a golden bowl, and a poem she had written herself.

---

## Before You Begin

This is the founding story of the Chinese *minghun* (冥婚, "underworld marriage") tradition in literature. It is not the earliest — there are older fragments — but it is the version that Chinese readers from the fourth century onward have carried in their heads as the shape the story should take. Gan Bao, who placed it in Book XVI of *Soushen Ji* (the book he reserved for encounters with the dead), tells it as if he had received it from a family that still existed in his own time and still remembered which grandmother had brought which child home from the grave.

The three figures we are about to meet:

- **Lu Chong** (卢充): a young man of the Fanyang (范阳) commandery, twenty years old, unmarried, from a family whose father had died when Lu Chong was small. Not a scholar. Not an official. A hunter — the kind of young provincial man who spent his afternoons in the woods west of the family compound and expected nothing supernatural to happen there.[^fanyang]
- **Cui Shaofu** (崔少府): a former Han official of the rank *Shàofǔ* (少府, "Junior Minister of the Household"), one of the Nine Chamberlains of the Han court. Dead — how long dead, Gan Bao does not say. Buried in a tomb thirty *li* west of the Lu family compound. In this story, he is not a ghost in the frightening sense; he is a courteous host receiving a son-in-law who has been expected.[^shaofu]
- **The Cui daughter** (崔氏女): unnamed. Also dead. In the story she is the bride, the mother of Lu Chong's son, the composer of a small classical Chinese poem, and the returner of the child. She has more agency, in her three brief scenes, than most living wives get in a Han-dynasty narrative.

One more thing before we begin. This is not a horror story. Gan Bao is not trying to frighten you. The Cui manor is not haunted; it is *administered*. Every step of the encounter — the guard at the gate, the change of clothes, the presentation of the marriage letter, the three-day feast, the parting instructions about the eventual child — follows the standard procedural forms of a Han-dynasty aristocratic wedding. What makes the story extraordinary is not that any single element is wrong, but that all of them are being performed *by the dead*. Gan Bao's premise, which he will not spell out but expects you to feel, is that the Han official class continued its rituals in the tomb, and that a marriage arranged between two fathers — one living, one dead — remained binding when one of the two children turned out to be dead as well. The bureaucracy of the ancestors was, in his cosmos, still open for business.

## The Story

### The Deer That Rose Again

The Lu family compound lay in the commandery of **Fanyang** (范阳), north of the Yellow River, on the fertile plain that runs between the Taihang range and the sea. It was a household of the middling gentry: not court officials, but landholders substantial enough to keep horses and to be entitled to hunt on their own western fields. **Lu Chong** was the young master of the house. His father had died when he was still small, and the responsibility of the compound had fallen to his mother and to an uncle. He had grown up watched over by household elders and left mostly to his own occupations, which in his twentieth year meant hunting.

The day before the **winter solstice** — the *dōng-zhì* (冬至), the shortest day of the year, when in the old ritual calendar the *yin* forces of the dead reach their annual peak and the barrier between worlds thins — Lu Chong took his bow and rode west of the compound to shoot game for the coming festival.[^dongzhi]

Three *li* out he sighted a **roebuck** — a *zhāng* (獐), the small dark deer of the northern woods — grazing on the edge of a copse. He nocked an arrow and let it fly. The arrow struck. The buck fell.

Then the buck stood up again.

Lu Chong had been hunting since he was twelve years old. He knew a mortal shot when he saw one; he had put the arrow through the animal, and the animal had gone down, and now the animal was on its feet and running west along the ridge. He kicked his horse into a gallop and gave chase.

He rode farther than he had ever ridden west of the compound. The sun crossed the sky. The buck stayed just ahead of him, always visible, never quite catchable, moving with the untroubled pace of an animal that was not really wounded. Lu Chong lost track of the distance. He lost track of the direction. When at last the buck vanished — not into cover, but simply out of his sight, as if it had walked through the air — he pulled up his horse and looked around.

He was on a road he had never seen. To the north of the road, perhaps a *li* away, stood a compound of high walls and tiled roofs, with a great gate and outbuildings on four sides, laid out in the manner of a senior official's residence. There had been no such compound west of Fanyang the last time he rode this way. There had been no such road.

The deer was gone. The compound was there.

### The Manor and the Letter

Lu Chong rode to the gate. A **bell-guard** — a *língxià* (铃下), the ranked servant who announced arrivals at the gates of Han officials — stood in the shadow of the portal.

"Guest arriving," the bell-guard called into the compound. Then, to Lu Chong: "Please, sir. Enter."

"Whose residence is this?" Lu Chong asked.

"The Junior Minister's residence."

Lu Chong looked down at himself. He had ridden hard through brush. His hunting tunic was streaked with sap and sweat; his boots were spattered. "My clothes are foul," he said. "I cannot present myself to a Junior Minister in this state."

The bell-guard did not answer. Instead, a household attendant emerged from the gate carrying a folded bundle. "The Master," the attendant said, "sends these garments to the young gentleman."

The bundle contained a full set of new clothes, folded in the style of a formal reception: an inner robe of unbleached silk, an outer robe of dark figured cloth, a fresh belt, cloth shoes. Lu Chong stepped behind the gate-screen, stripped off his hunting things, and put the new clothes on. They fit as if they had been measured for him.

He was led through two courtyards to a hall where an elderly man in the black-and-purple court robes of a former **Shàofǔ** sat waiting. The man rose. Lu Chong gave his name. The man returned the greeting with the formality of a court audience and gestured him to a seat.

Wine was brought. Roast meat. The two men ate and drank in silence for several rounds — the standard opening of a Han social visit, in which nothing important is said until the third or fourth cup. Then Cui Shaofu set his cup down and spoke.

"Your late honored father," he said, "did not consider my household beneath his dignity. Some time ago I received a letter from him. In it, he asked for the hand of my daughter — for you. That is why I have called you here."

He drew from his sleeve a folded sheet of silk. He held it out.

Lu Chong opened it.

His father had died when Lu Chong was six years old. Lu Chong remembered the man only in fragments — the smell of a certain kind of ink, the shape of a hand holding a brush, the sound of a voice reading aloud in the evening. He did not remember the man's face. But he remembered the hand. His father had been a moderately educated man of the Han provincial gentry, and his brushwork had a particular character that Lu Chong's mother had shown him, one letter at a time, in the years after the death.

The letter Cui Shaofu was holding out had been written by his father. Lu Chong recognized the strokes before he had finished the first line. He read the request. It was courteous, unhurried, entirely in his father's cadence. It was dated to a year Lu Chong could not immediately fix but which was, by any reasonable accounting, after his father had died.

He felt his breath catch. The classical text says only *歔欷 xūxī* — a stifled sob, the sound of a man trying not to weep in public. He handed the letter back.

He did not refuse the marriage. It would have been an impossible thing to refuse: his father, in his own hand, had asked for it. He inclined his head.

Cui Shaofu turned and called into the inner rooms: **"The young gentleman has come. The young lady may now prepare herself."** Then, to Lu Chong, more gently: "You may withdraw to the east veranda. Wait there."

### The Three-Day Wedding

Lu Chong waited on the east veranda until dusk. When the light had almost gone, a servant announced that the young lady was ready.

He was brought to the wedding mat.

She had already stepped down from her bridal carriage. She stood at the head of the mat in her wedding clothes, her face powdered in the Han fashion, her hair set in the double coils of a bride. Lu Chong made the four ceremonial bows. She returned them. They knelt opposite each other on the mat. The household elders, from behind a screen, spoke the standard formulas.

They were married.

For three days he stayed in the Cui compound as a bridegroom. The Han wedding rite runs three days in the strict form — three days of feasts, three days of introductions to the household, three days at the end of which the new couple emerges as one household. Lu Chong ate at the Cui tables. He was introduced to the Cui uncles and cousins. He slept beside his new wife in the eastern chamber. She spoke to him softly. She did not tell him anything about herself that he could later use to place her.

On the fourth morning Cui Shaofu called him to the front hall.

"You may return home now," the old man said. "My daughter is with child. If she bears a son, we will return him to you — do not doubt this. If she bears a daughter, the girl will remain here and be raised in this house."

He ordered the household carriage brought.

Lu Chong made his farewells. His wife stood behind her father's shoulder. She did not speak. She looked at him with an attention that was neither cheerful nor sad. Cui Shaofu walked him to the middle gate, took him by the hand, and wept.

Outside the gate stood a **calf-drawn cart** with a driver in dark green, and beside the cart Lu Chong's own hunting clothes and bow and arrows, folded and laid on top as if they had never left. Another attendant hurried out with a second bundle of gifts — a full set of clothes, a set of quilts, a bedding cover — and pressed them into the cart, saying, on the old man's behalf: *"The bond has only just begun and the parting is bitter. Please accept these things."*

Lu Chong climbed into the cart. The driver flicked the reins. The cart moved — and moved fast — and in what the classical text describes as *去如电逝*, *departed like lightning*, he was home.

His mother came running out of the compound gate. She had thought him lost. He climbed down from a cart no one in the household recognized, wearing clothes no one had made for him, holding bundles no one had packed. He told them what had happened.

They asked, gently, whose manor he had described.

He told them: Cui Shaofu, of the estate thirty *li* west, in the compound with the tile roofs and the high gate.

There was a silence. Then his uncle said the thing that everyone in the household had already understood.

Cui Shaofu had died thirty years ago. The compound Lu Chong had described was his **tomb**.

Lu Chong sat down. The classical text says only *追以懊惋 zhuī yǐ àowǎn* — *he was seized, in retrospect, by grief and confusion*. He had married a dead woman, in a dead man's house, with a letter written by his own dead father. And he had, apparently, done so correctly. The wedding gifts were real. The bruises the cart had left on his shoulders were real. Whatever had happened, it had not been a dream.

### The Waterside, Four Years On

For four years nothing else happened.

Lu Chong told the story once and then, at his mother's request, stopped telling it. He put the bridal gifts away in a locked chest. He did not marry a living woman in that time, though his uncles suggested it. He waited without knowing what he was waiting for.

On the **third day of the third month** in the fourth year — the festival of *shàngsì* (上巳), when it was the old northern custom to go to the riverside and wash off the accumulated bad luck of the past year — Lu Chong went with the household to the water.[^shangsi]

The party spread out along the bank. Someone pointed downstream.

Out on the water — not moving toward the bank, not moving away — two **calf-drawn carts** were floating, half-sinking and half-rising, in a rhythm that had nothing to do with the current. The whole party saw them. This is a point Gan Bao emphasizes: *同坐皆见*, *everyone present saw*. It was not a private vision.

The carts drifted nearer. Lu Chong walked to the water's edge, waded out until he could reach the rear door of the nearer cart, and opened it.

Inside sat the Cui daughter. On her knee sat a small boy of about three years old.

She looked at him with the same attention as on the parting morning. She said his name. He said hers. She lifted the child down and set him on the plank floor of the cart.

Then she reached under the seat and drew out a **golden bowl** — a heavy, plain, unornamented drinking bowl of the kind that in the Han funerary tradition would be placed at the head of a coffin as an offering to the spirit of the dead — and handed it across to him along with a folded sheet of silk on which she had written a poem in her own hand. The poem is preserved in the *Soushen Ji* text; its central image is that of a wild goose calling once at the boundary of two rivers and then flying on.[^parting-poem]

She said: *"This son I return to you, as my father promised. Raise him well. The bowl is yours. Sell it if you must; it will find its own way to the right hands."*

Then she closed the door of the cart. The two carts drifted back out into the water. They sank, and did not rise.

Lu Chong walked out of the river carrying a living three-year-old boy in one arm and a heavy bowl in the other.

### The Bowl at the Market

The boy grew. His grandmother — Lu Chong's mother — raised him, at first uncertainly, then, when it became clear that the child ate ordinary food and cast an ordinary shadow and had a face that resembled both his father and, disquietingly, his father's father, with a devotion that closed over any remaining doubt. His name, in the family record Gan Bao is preserving, was **Wenxiu** (温休).

When Wenxiu was a few years older, Lu Chong took the golden bowl to market in the county seat. The family had money, but the bowl had been given to him with a purpose, and he intended to see the purpose worked out. He set the bowl on a mat in the marketplace.

An **old woman** walked past, stopped, and picked it up. She turned it in her hands. Her face changed. She asked him, quietly, where he had come by it.

He told her, as briefly as he could, that it had been given to him by a young woman at the waterside.

The old woman said — Gan Bao gives her the line — *"This bowl came from my niece's grave."*

She was of the **Cui family**. She was Cui Shaofu's sister. She had, thirty years ago, helped bury her niece — the daughter of Cui Shaofu, who had died at nineteen, unmarried and without issue — and she had, with her own hands, placed this bowl at the head of the coffin as a grave-good.

She went to the Cui family. The senior Cui uncles, who were the reigning heads of the household in Lu Chong's own time, came to see him. They came, in the tentative way of a family that has heard a story it cannot afford to disbelieve, to look at the child.

The child was unmistakably a Cui. The senior aunt — who had been alive when Cui Shaofu's daughter was alive, and who had known that girl since her birth — took the boy in her arms and wept. The Cui family opened its record book and entered the boy's name into the line.

Wenxiu grew up between two households: the Lu compound where he had been raised, and the Cui compound where he was, from that day onward, a recognized descendant. He was educated by both families. He rose through the provincial examinations. In the surviving genealogical accounts of the northern Cui line, which was one of the great aristocratic houses of the later Han and the Six Dynasties, Wenxiu appears as a **magistrate** in his prime and as a **provincial commander** in his later years. His descendants held office for four generations after him.

That, Gan Bao says, is why the story is worth writing down. It is not because a young man once shot a deer and followed it into a tomb. It is because a great Northern line owed its founding to a marriage in which one of the two mothers had been dead for thirty years, and no one in the Han official class of that region felt able to deny the fact.

## Translator's Reflection

Two things about this story keep me returning to it.

The first is the **letter**. Cui Shaofu could have arranged the marriage on his own authority — he is the senior party in the story, he holds the manor, he holds the daughter, and Lu Chong is a twenty-year-old provincial with no way to refuse. He does not need the letter. But Gan Bao gives him the letter, and the letter is in Lu Chong's own father's hand, dated after the father's death.

The classical Chinese cosmos, in this story, is one in which the dead continue their business. Cui Shaofu, dead, has been maintaining correspondence with Lu Chong's father, also dead, about the marriage prospects of their children. The two fathers have been doing what fathers of aristocratic Chinese households have always done — arranging the next generation's alliances — from opposite sides of the veil. Lu Chong is not conscripted into a supernatural marriage against his will. He is being handed the result of a correspondence he was never party to but which he is, by the standards of both families' ancestor cults, absolutely bound by. When he recognizes his father's hand and stifles a sob and says nothing, he is not surrendering to a ghost. He is accepting the standing filial obligation of every Han son: *your father, living or dead, is entitled to arrange your marriage, and you are entitled to weep about it but not to refuse.*

The second thing that keeps me returning to it is the **bowl**.

Gan Bao could have ended the story at the waterside — with the return of the child, the poem, the sinking cart. Many later versions of the tale, in fact, do end there. But Gan Bao goes on. He takes us to the marketplace. He introduces the old woman who happens to walk past, who happens to look down, who happens to recognize the bowl she herself placed in her niece's grave thirty years ago.

The bowl is what makes the story *testable*. Everything else in Lu Chong's account could be dismissed as a young man's fever dream: the manor, the letter, the wedding, the calf-cart, the water. But the bowl exists. It has weight. It came out of a specific known grave, placed there by a specific known woman, and it has now arrived in a marketplace in the hand of the young man who claims to have been given it by the dead girl. The Cui family can send for the coffin, if they must, and confirm the absence of the bowl. They do not seem to have needed to. The old woman recognized it on sight. The identification held.

The bowl is Gan Bao's answer to the skeptical Han-dynasty reader — the reader who wants a story like this to remain in the domain of the imagination. He is telling that reader: *no, this one has a physical object attached to it. The bowl is in the Cui household record. The bowl went out of a grave and into a marketplace and into a genealogy.* You can, if you doubt me, go and look at the record.

This is the move that makes the *Soushen Ji* the *Soushen Ji* rather than a fairy-tale collection. Gan Bao believed that the supernatural, when it happened, left evidence. The letters were written. The clothes were folded. The bowls came home. And in the end the whole northern Cui line, one of the most powerful aristocratic houses of the next four hundred years, traced itself to a marriage that had happened, in his account, exactly the way he described it.

Whether he was right, we cannot now know. But he wrote it down as if he were writing down the founding of a house — because to him, that is what it was.

[^fanyang]: 范阳 (Fànyáng) was a commandery of the Han and later of the Wei-Jin period, occupying roughly the area of present-day Zhuozhou and Baoding in Hebei, on the plain south of the Yan mountains. In Gan Bao's own day it was already famous as the seat of the **Northern Cui** (北崔) aristocratic clan, whose ancestors' tombs dotted the countryside west of the county town. Placing Lu Chong "thirty *li* west of the Cui tomb" is a deliberate act of geographical anchoring by Gan Bao: any fourth-century reader from the region would have understood immediately which tomb was meant, and the ability of a local reader to visit the mound was part of what gave the story its authority.

[^shaofu]: 少府 (*Shàofǔ*, "Junior Minister of the Household") was one of the *Jiǔ-qīng* (九卿, Nine Chamberlains) of the Han court, in charge of the imperial private treasury and household services. It was a senior post; a retired Shaofu returning to the provinces would have brought back court rank, household staff, and the resources to build the kind of walled compound with tile roofs and multi-courtyard layout that Lu Chong sees. That Gan Bao gives Cui the title but does not name him further is characteristic: he is signaling *social level* rather than making a claim about a specific historical figure, and leaving the door open for later Cui genealogists to fit the account into their own family record.

[^dongzhi]: 冬至 (*Dōng-zhì*), the winter solstice, was one of the two most ritually charged days in the Han calendar (the other being the summer solstice). In the Han cosmology, it marked the moment when the *yīn* forces of the earth and the dead reached their annual peak and began to yield to the returning *yáng*. Hunting on the eve of the solstice was traditional in the northern gentry households — game was needed for the ancestor offerings — but it was also considered a liminal night, on which the boundaries between the mortal roads and the spirit roads were softest. Gan Bao's placement of Lu Chong's hunt on this specific day is not decorative. It is the story's cosmological hinge.

[^shangsi]: 上巳 (*Shàng-sì*) was the festival of the third day of the third lunar month, celebrated in the Han and Six Dynasties by ritual bathing at rivers and streams — the ceremony known as *fú xì* (祓禊), "washing away the ill-luck." It was one of the *yáng* festivals, in which the daylight world was thought to open at its brightest and the boundary between worlds thinned in the *other* direction — permitting spirits to come out to the human world in daylight, without needing the cover of dream. Gan Bao's decision to place the return of the Cui daughter on this specific day balances the story's cosmology: winter solstice (Yin peak) called Lu Chong into the tomb; spring bathing festival (Yang peak) returns the child to him at the river. The whole tale is bracketed by the two great annual ritual openings of the boundary.

[^parting-poem]: The parting poem attributed to the Cui daughter is preserved in several early manuscript lines of *Soushen Ji*. Its central figure is the *lone wild goose* (孤雁) calling once at the confluence of two rivers before flying on — a Han-dynasty poetic image for a woman who has fulfilled a duty and must now leave without lingering. Later editors of the story sometimes shortened the poem to its opening couplet, but Gan Bao appears to have given the full text. That a dead girl of nineteen composes a formally correct Han four-word verse in her own hand and hands it to her living husband along with a grave-good bowl is, in Gan Bao's telling, precisely the kind of detail that would have made a Han audience *believe* the account: educated girls of the Cui rank did in fact compose such poems in life, and the surviving one, transmitted by the story, was checkable against the family's own written record.

<summary>🏛️ Historical Context · 历史背景</summary>

**Gan Bao (干宝, c. 286–336)** was a court archivist and historian of the early Eastern Jin. He served as the imperial *Zhùzuò Láng* (著作郎) — the officer in charge of drafting the dynastic history — and he assembled *Soushen Ji* (搜神记, *In Search of the Supernatural*) both as an official-adjacent literary project and as a personal answer to two supernatural events in his own family: a sister who returned from apparent death, and a family maidservant who was found alive after being sealed in a tomb. Book XVI of the collection, in which *Lu Chong* appears, is the volume in which he gathered his most extended accounts of encounters between the living and the dead — the stories he thought worth telling at length rather than in the compressed one-paragraph form of the shorter tales.

**Book XVI and the ghost-marriage tradition.** Before Gan Bao, the ghost-marriage (*mínghūn*, 冥婚) was already a real social practice in Han and post-Han China: families with an unmarried daughter or son who had died prematurely would sometimes arrange posthumous marriages, often with an effigy standing in for one of the parties, so that the dead child would not be lonely in the ancestor line. What Gan Bao does with the practice in *Lu Chong* is different from the social ritual. He tells a story in which the marriage is arranged *between* two dead fathers, executed *by* a dead daughter, and consummated with a *living* son — and in which the offspring of that marriage is a living child who founds a real historical line. In doing so, he gives Chinese narrative literature its founding template for what later ages would call the *rén-guǐ hūn-liàn* (人鬼婚恋, "human-ghost marriage-love") genre. Every subsequent Tang *chuanqi* about a scholar meeting a dead girl on the road, every Ming and Qing romance in which a ghost bears a mortal child, every Pu Songling story about a scholar marrying a girl who turns out to have died young — all of them are working in the shape Gan Bao gave the genre here.

**The Northern Cui as historical context.** The **Northern Cui of Fanyang** (范阳北崔) were one of the two or three most powerful aristocratic clans of the Northern and Southern Dynasties, holding senior court and provincial offices in almost every generation from the late Han through the early Tang. That Gan Bao attaches the origin of one of the Cui branches to a ghost marriage is not throwaway. The story is, among other things, a *founding narrative* — the kind of account a great house is willing to have told about its origins because the account confers a certain kind of legitimacy. To descend from a match arranged in the tomb of a Han Junior Minister, sealed by a letter from a father in the underworld, and confirmed by a grave-good bowl that surfaced in a marketplace, was a genealogy no rival house could easily challenge.

**On the bowl.** The *golden bowl* — 金碗, *jīn wǎn* — of the story is a specific Han funerary artifact type: a heavy plain drinking bowl of gilt or solid gold, placed at the head of an aristocratic coffin as an offering-vessel for the spirit. Archaeological finds from Han tombs of the Fanyang region regularly include such bowls, and Gan Bao's original readers would have understood immediately what class of object was meant. That the bowl is *unornamented* is deliberate: an ornamented bowl might have been dismissed as a common trade piece, but a plain grave-bowl of a specific weight and finish could be identified by the family member who had commissioned it. The story's evidentiary logic depends on this: the bowl is not a symbol, it is *evidence*.

**On the manuscript tradition.** The *Soushen Ji* we have is a Ming-dynasty reconstruction by Hu Yinglin (胡应麟) and later scholars, working from the fragments preserved in the Tang and Song encyclopedias — principally the *Taiping Yulan* (太平御览) and the *Taiping Guangji* (太平广记). *Lu Chong* is one of the entries that survives essentially intact across multiple recensions, because it was quoted at length by every major medieval compiler. Two lines in particular — Cui Shaofu's *"Your late honored father did not consider my house beneath his dignity"*, and the Cui daughter's silent handing over of the bowl — appear in more than a dozen medieval sources and can be reconstructed with high confidence. What we read is very close to what Gan Bao wrote.
